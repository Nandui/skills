# Instagram Post Optimizer

Turn a photo (or carousel, or Reel cover) into a full, ready-to-post package that's tuned to *this* creator and *this* goal: an on-image hook, a caption with variants, tiered hashtags, and a CTA with posting tips.

The single biggest lever on quality is **knowing who the post is for**. The same beach selfie becomes a completely different post for a calm "digital big sister" advice account, a chaotic gym-comedy page, or a luxury-travel brand — different hook, different voice, different hashtags, different CTA. A caption written without that context is generic, and generic is exactly what the algorithm and the audience scroll past. So the first job is always to ground yourself in the persona, then in the image, then write.

---

## Step 1 — Know who the post is for

Before writing anything, establish the persona. In order of preference:

1. **A persona brief** (best). If the user has one — a niche/audience/voice doc, a `SOUL.md`, brand guidelines, or even a few lines they paste — use it. If they've mentioned one earlier in the conversation or in project memory, use that. A good brief covers: who the account is, the audience, the niche/content lanes, the voice/tone, and what they're trying to grow.
2. **An @handle or profile link.** If that's all you have, attempt light research with web tools to learn the niche and voice. Be honest about limits — Instagram usually blocks profile scraping behind a login wall, so you often can't read their posts directly. If you can't learn enough, don't guess silently: ask.
3. **Ask, briefly.** If you have neither, ask 3-4 tight questions and nothing more: *Who's the account / what's the niche? Who's the audience? What voice (e.g. calm & direct, playful, luxe, unhinged-funny)? What are you trying to grow — reach, engagement, followers, or clicks?* Keep it to one message.

Don't stall the whole task hunting for a perfect brief. Once you know enough to write in the right voice for the right audience, move on.

**Why this matters enough to ask:** voice is the difference between a caption that sounds like the creator and one that sounds like a brand bot. Audience determines which references, hashtags, and CTAs actually land. Get this wrong and everything downstream is wasted effort.

---

## Step 2 — Read the image(s)

Actually look at what you were given. Note:

- **Subject & literal content** — who/what, setting, outfit, action, props, any text already on the image.
- **Mood & story** — what feeling or moment it conveys; what a viewer would assume or feel.
- **Hook potential** — the most arresting, curious, or relatable thing in the frame. This is what the on-image text and caption opener should grab onto.
- **Existing text** — if there's already text on the image, complement it, don't duplicate it.
- **Carousel arc** — for multiple images, read them as a sequence: what's the cover, what's the payoff, what makes someone swipe.

The caption must feel connected to what's actually in the picture. A great line that could sit on any photo is weaker than a specific one that could only sit on *this* one.

---

## Step 3 — Set the goal (suggest, then let the user steer)

Different goals demand different captions and CTAs, so pin one **primary** goal per post (a single clear CTA beats three competing ones). If the user already named a goal, use it. Otherwise, **recommend** the goal that best fits the image + persona in one line, deliver the package for it, and offer a one-line switch (e.g. *"Angled this for reach/saves — say the word and I'll re-cut it for comments or followers."*). Only stop to ask if it's genuinely a toss-up.

| Goal | Caption strategy | Typical CTA |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Reach / virality** | Saves + shares are the strongest reach signals. Make it *save-worthy* (useful, list-y, "I needed this") or *share-worthy* (relatable, "this is so me", tag-a-friend). Tight, quotable. | "Save this for later" · "Send this to someone who…" |
| **Engagement** | End on an easy, specific prompt that's fast to answer — a this-or-that, a fill-in-the-blank, a hot take that invites disagreement. | "Which one — 1 or 2?" · "Tell me I'm wrong 👇" |
| **Follower growth** | Signal identity + recurring value: who this is for and why following pays off again next time. Strong POV. | "Follow for [specific recurring value]" |
| **Clicks / conversions** | One clear value prop, one destination. Strip competing CTAs. Build a little curiosity gap to the link/DM. | "Link in bio" · "DM me '[word]'" |

## Step 4 — Write the package

Match the persona's voice throughout. Use these craft rules because they reflect how Instagram actually surfaces and rewards posts today — not arbitrary formatting:

- **Embody the persona's energy; never narrate it.** Traits like "big sister," "mentor," "wise," "luxury," "hype girl" describe the *vibe the post should radiate* — they are not labels to write into the copy. Don't state the role ("let big sis tell you…", "as your hype girl…") and don't spell out the moral of the post. Both read as cringe and try-hard and instantly turn a caption into a motivational poster. The energy comes through tone, confidence, and word choice while the caption itself stays effortless and light — often short beats a paragraph of advice. The matching failure on the other side is flattening a warm/cute/humble brief into generic "edgy empowerment / girlboss" one-liners ("too much? good"). Aim for neither: write like *that specific person* would actually caption it. When bridging a persona onto off-brand image content (e.g. an advice persona posting an outfit), change the *topic*, not the *soul* — keep the vibe, lose the lecture.
- **Sound like a real person, not a brand — this is the one that matters most.** The fastest way to read as fake is the influencer-caption formula: clever hook + a description of the outfit/photo + a "save this / tag a friend" CTA + a cluster of emojis. Real people do not caption their own photos that way. They're casual, specific, often understated or a little mundane — lowercase, loose punctuation, an inside joke, one tiny real detail ("wore my sister's boots again, she doesn't know yet"), sometimes just two words. They don't explain the picture or announce their intent. For persona accounts especially, an authentic-sounding caption *is* the optimization — so keep the machinery subtle or out of the caption entirely: hashtags can go in the first comment, the CTA can be implied or dropped, on-image text isn't mandatory. When torn between a polished line and a plain-but-real one, pick real. When in doubt, write less and make it more specific to this exact moment.
- **Front-load the hook** (when the caption is doing a job — advice, story, value). Only the first ~1 line shows before "…more", and on-image text is what stops the scroll in the first place. Put the strongest idea first; never open with a throat-clear ("So today I wanted to…").
- **On-image / cover text is the scroll-stopper.** Keep it to ~3-7 words, high-contrast and legible at thumbnail size. For carousels, the cover hook is make-or-break; for Reels, write the cover-frame hook.
- **Caption length follows the goal.** Short and punchy for reach; a few short lines of story or value for engagement/connection; benefit-led for conversions. Break lines for breathing room — walls of text get skipped.
- **Write for IG search, lightly.** Instagram indexes caption and alt text, so weave in 1-2 natural keywords someone might search for the topic. Don't keyword-stuff.
- **Hashtags: relevance over volume.** The 30-generic-tags era is over and reads as spammy. Use ~8-15 *relevant* tags in a mix of sizes so the post can rank in smaller pools before fighting in giant ones: a few niche (small, specific communities), several medium, a couple broad/topical. Skip banned, dead, or wildly off-topic tags. Caption or first comment both work — pick one.
- **CTA: one, and earn it.** Tie it to the goal. Avoid guideline-risky engagement bait ("comment to win", "like if…") — it gets downranked and looks desperate. A good CTA feels like a natural next step.
- **Emojis & formatting on-brand.** Tasteful and matched to voice; a calm advice persona uses them sparingly, a playful one more freely.

### Output template

Deliver in this shape, copy-paste friendly:

```
## [Single photo / Carousel / Reel cover] — optimized for [goal] ([one-line why])

### On-image hook text
- A: "[≤7 words]"
- B: "[≤7 words]"
[Carousel: cover hook + a short line per key slide. Reel: cover-frame hook.]

### Caption — primary
[Hook line]
[Body: 1-3 short lines, in persona voice]
[CTA line]

### Caption — variant 2 ([angle, e.g. "relatable/funny"])
[…]

### Caption — variant 3 ([angle, e.g. "story-led"])
[…]

### Hashtags  (~8-15, tiered — paste as-is)
#niche1 #niche2 #niche3 #medium1 #medium2 #medium3 #medium4 #broad1 #broad2
(one line: the mix you chose and why)

### Posting tips
- CTA: [the chosen CTA, if not already in the caption]
- Best time: [sensible default for the audience + "check your own analytics"]
- First comment: [seed a question or park the hashtags here]
- Alt text: "[plain description of the image for accessibility + reach]"
- Location tag: [suggestion, or "skip"]
```

Keep variants genuinely different — different *angle* (e.g. relatable vs. story-led vs. bold-statement), not the same caption reworded. That gives the user a real choice.

---

## Edge cases

- **No persona info and the user won't give it:** infer a reasonable voice from the image + @handle, *state the assumption in one line*, and write confidently. Offer to sharpen it with a brief.
- **Sensitive or personal images:** stay respectful; don't manufacture drama for engagement.
- **Faceless / product / aesthetic shots:** lean on mood, utility, and SEO keywords rather than personality.
- **Language:** write in the persona's / audience's language (default English; switch if the brief or handle implies otherwise).
- **Don't fabricate data:** never invent follower counts, "best time" stats as if measured, or trending claims you can't support. Frame timing as a sensible default to verify against their analytics.

## Worked example

**Persona brief:** "Maya — calm 'big sister' dating-advice account for women in their 20s. Voice: warm, direct, quotable, never preachy. Growing saves + shares."
**Image:** a soft, golden-hour selfie, slight knowing smile.

```
## Single photo — optimized for reach/saves (relatable + quotable suits this persona's save-driven growth)

### On-image hook text
- A: "Stop texting first. Watch."
- B: "The 3-day rule, but make it self-respect"

### Caption — primary
You are not "too much." You were just over-explaining yourself to people who decided not to understand.
Save this for the next time you're tempted to send the paragraph.
🤍

### Caption — variant 2 (bold one-liner)
Closure is a word you give yourself, not a text you wait for.
Send this to the friend who's still drafting it.

### Caption — variant 3 (story-led)
Spent my whole early 20s mistaking anxiety for chemistry.
Calm is not boring. Calm is safe. Calm is the upgrade.
Follow along if no one ever taught you that.

### Hashtags (~10, tiered)
#datingadviceforwomen #selfworthjourney #healingera #datingin20s #womensupportingwomen #relationshiptips #emotionalmaturity #selfrespect #datingadvice #mindsetshift
(3 niche / 4 medium / 3 broad — ranks in the smaller dating-advice pools first, then reaches wider)

### Posting tips
- CTA: "Save this for later" (already in the caption)
- Best time: evenings ~7-9pm when her audience is winding down and scrolling — confirm against her analytics
- First comment: "What's the lie you believed about love in your early 20s? 👇"
- Alt text: "Woman in golden-hour light with a soft, knowing smile; calm, confident mood."
- Location tag: skip — content is universal, no place adds reach here
```
